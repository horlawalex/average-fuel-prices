The Evolution of Fuel Prices in Nigeria

Over the past ten years, fuel prices in Nigeria have undergone significant transformations, shaped by both local and global factors. From 2018 to 2024, the costs of petrol (PMS) and diesel have risen steadily, punctuated by policy shifts, economic reforms, and infrastructural developments. These changes have had far-reaching consequences, affecting transportation, businesses, and the cost of living.

The most notable change occurred in June 2023 when the federal government announced the removal of fuel subsidies., resulting in a sharp surge in petrol prices. This policy change was introduced to curb government spending but placed a heavy financial burden on citizens and businesses alike. Similarly, diesel prices have climbed due to increased demand and limited domestic refining capacity.

The Ripple Effects of Escalating Fuel Costs in Nigeria

The sharp and continuous increase in fuel prices in Nigeria has reverberated across various sectors of the economy, touching every aspect of daily life. From transportation to food prices, and industrial production to household expenses, the economic effects are both far-reaching and deeply felt.

Transportation Costs on the Rise

As fuel prices soar, transportation costs have surged, disproportionately impacting commuters and businesses reliant on logistics. Data from various states indicates that public transport fares have doubled or tripled in many urban areas. For instance, the fare for a common route in Lagos, which was around ₦200 in 2021, has risen to ₦600 - ₦800 by late 2024. This has placed an immense financial burden on workers who rely on daily commuting. Similarly, the rise in diesel prices has significantly driven up freight and delivery costs across Nigeria. Reports indicate that transporting goods between major commercial hubs, such as Lagos and Kano, has become considerably more expensive since the removal of fuel subsidies in June 2023. This increase has disrupted supply chains and contributed to higher retail prices for goods nationwide.

Rising Inflation and Household Burdens

Fuel prices are a major driver of inflation in Nigeria. The removal of fuel subsidies in 2023 has had a cascading effect on the cost of goods and services. Nigeria’s inflation rate, which was 15.6% in January 2023, climbed to over 28% by October 2024, according to the National Bureau of Statistics (NBS). A significant portion of this increase is attributed to higher transportation and production costs tied to fuel price hikes. The average Nigerian household now spends over 60% of income on food and transportation, leaving little for education, healthcare, or savings. For low-income families, this economic pressure is pushing many below the poverty line.

Energy and Production Challenges

With a notoriously unreliable power grid, many businesses rely on diesel-powered generators to sustain operations. Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs), which make up 96% of Nigerian businesses, are particularly vulnerable. Diesel costs have increased by over 150% between 2022 and 2024, forcing many businesses to either cut production or close entirely. For instance, a bakery in Abuja that spent ₦500,000 monthly on diesel in 2022 now spends ₦1.3 million, reducing profitability and leading to layoffs. Manufacturing and agriculture, which are energy-intensive sectors, have also been severely affected. Rising energy costs are driving up production costs, contributing to higher prices for domestically produced goods.

A Historical View of Fuel Pricing

Fuel prices in Nigeria have historically been a contentious issue, reflecting policy decisions and global oil market dynamics. Between 1991 and 2021, the average price hovered around 0.43 USD per liter, with notable highs and lows. However, the post-2023 era has introduced unprecedented price levels, redefining the landscape of energy affordability in Nigeria. Following the removal of subsidies, petrol prices rose from ₦185 per liter in early 2023 to ₦600-₦700 in mid-2023, and now range between ₦855 and ₦1,030 per liter in late 2024. Diesel prices have similarly skyrocketed, reaching an average of ₦1,379 per liter in November 2024.

Conclusion: The Road Ahead

Addressing the challenges posed by rising fuel prices will require a multifaceted approach. Beyond the Dangote Refinery, investment in additional local refineries is critical to reducing dependency on imports and stabilizing supply. Renewable energy investments, such as solar and wind, can diversify Nigeria’s energy mix, reducing reliance on fossil fuels and their volatile prices. Policies like conditional cash transfers or transportation subsidies can cushion the effects of rising costs on low-income families. Initiatives to promote energy-saving practices and technologies can reduce overall fuel consumption and economic strain.
In the coming years, achieving energy affordability and stability will hinge on the effective implementation of these strategies. While the Dangote Refinery represents a step forward, broader reforms and investments are necessary to secure Nigeria's energy future.
