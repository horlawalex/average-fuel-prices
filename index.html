The Evolution of Fuel Prices in Nigeria

Over the past decade, fuel prices in Nigeria have undergone significant transformations, shaped by both local and global factors. From 2018 to 2024, the costs of petrol (PMS) and diesel have risen steadily, punctuated by policy shifts, economic reforms, and infrastructural developments. These changes have had far-reaching consequences, affecting transportation, businesses, and the cost of living.

The most notable change occurred in June 2023 when the federal government announced the removal of fuel subsidies, resulting in a sharp surge in petrol prices. This policy change was introduced to curb government spending but placed a heavy financial burden on citizens and businesses alike. Similarly, diesel prices have climbed due to increased demand and limited domestic refining capacity.

The Ripple Effects of Escalating Fuel Costs in Nigeria

The sharp and continuous increase in fuel prices in Nigeria has disrupted daily life, with ripple effects touching nearly every sector of the economy. From skyrocketing transportation costs that burden commuters to escalating food prices straining household budgets, the impact is impossible to ignore. Businesses, both small and large, are grappling with rising operational costs, while families are forced to make difficult choices just to survive. These price hikes are more than numbers on a chart they reflect the daily struggles of millions of Nigerians trying to cope with a reality where everything feels increasingly out of reach.

Transportation Costs on the Rise

The sharp increase in fuel prices has triggered a corresponding surge in transportation costs, placing a heavy financial strain on commuters and businesses alike. Across various states, public transport fares have risen significantly in urban centers, cutting deeper into already stretched household budgets.

As fuel prices soar, transportation costs have surged, disproportionately impacting commuters and businesses reliant on logistics. In Abuja, fuel prices have now reached an average of 66 cents per liter, a 15% increase since September. This surge has placed a significant financial burden on workers, with some finding it unaffordable to commute. For example, Victor Zion was forced to quit his job because commuting costs consumed up to 70% of his monthly salary(VOANews)

Many workers, particularly in the informal sector, are struggling with the rising transportation costs. Some employers, like Moses Ogwoke, have resorted to covering part of their workers’ transportation costs to retain staff, but this has cut into business profits. (VOANews)

The increase in transportation costs is contributing to a destabilizing labor market, with many workers being unable to sustain themselves due to high commuting expenses. With the government’s promised solutions yet to materialize, many Nigerians are caught in a difficult situation: pay for transportation or resign from their jobs.

Rising Inflation and Household Burdens

Fuel prices are a major driver of inflation in Nigeria. The removal of fuel subsidies in 2023 has had a cascading effect on the cost of goods and services. Nigeria’s inflation rate, which was 15.6% in January 2023, climbed to over 28% by October 2024, according to the National Bureau of Statistics (NBS). A significant portion of this increase is attributed to higher transportation and production costs tied to fuel price hikes. The average Nigerian household now spends over 60% of income on food and transportation, leaving little for education, healthcare, or savings. For low-income families, this economic pressure is pushing many below the poverty line.

Energy and Production Challenges

Rising diesel costs in Nigeria are severely impacting businesses, with prices soaring from ₦312 per liter in January 2022 to ₦1,700 in March 2024, a staggering increase of over 400%. This sharp rise is straining industries reliant on diesel, such as trucking, construction, and manufacturing. Many businesses, like Juwon Adesanya’s trucking company, are seeing shrinking margins and struggling to remain profitable.

In addition, Nigeria’s unreliable power grid forces businesses to rely on costly diesel-powered generators. This drives up operational costs and disrupts productivity. Small manufacturers, in particular, are facing higher production costs, which are being passed on to consumers, further inflating the cost of living.

The Manufacturers Association of Nigeria (MAN) has highlighted high energy costs as the top challenge for the sector, compounded by issues such as limited access to raw materials and forex shortages. As diesel prices continue to rise, businesses are being forced to make difficult decisions, potentially raising prices or scaling back operations to survive.

A Historical View of Fuel Pricing

Fuel prices in Nigeria have historically been a contentious issue, reflecting policy decisions and global oil market dynamics. Between 1991 and 2021, the average price hovered around 0.43 USD per liter, with notable highs and lows. However, the post-2023 era has introduced unprecedented price levels, redefining the landscape of energy affordability in Nigeria. Following the removal of subsidies, petrol prices rose from ₦185 per liter in early 2023 to ₦600-₦700 in mid-2023, and now range between ₦855 and ₦1,030 per liter in late 2024. Diesel prices have similarly skyrocketed, reaching an average of ₦1,447 per liter in November 2024.

Conclusion: The Road Ahead

To effectively address the escalating challenges caused by rising fuel prices, the Nigerian government should look into sustainable solutions. Since the removal of fuel subsidies initially triggered the sharp increase in prices, the government has a responsibility to mitigate its effects by fostering an environment that reduces fuel costs for the population.

This includes prioritizing investments in additional local refineries to reduce reliance on fuel imports, stabilizing supply, and ensuring affordability. A significant reduction in fuel prices will have a ripple effect, bringing transportation costs, food prices, and overall living expenses back to reasonable levels, easing the economic burden on Nigerians.

Ultimately, achieving long-term stability in fuel prices requires deliberate government policies and reforms aimed at improving local production, promoting energy efficiency, and cushioning the impacts on low-income households.
